Old English vs. Middle English

Key Linguistic Transformations (c. 1150–1500)

I. Grammar (Morfología y Sintaxis)

The shift from a synthetic (inflected) to an analytic (fixed word order) language.

Feature Old English (c. 450–1150) Middle English (c. 1150–1500) Change Summary
Case System Highly inflected (4 main cases) marked by complex endings. Greatly simplified; most case endings lost. Genitive ('s) survives. Simplification of Inflections
Gender Grammatical Gender (nouns had masc., fem., or neut. gender). Natural Gender (gender based on sex). Grammatical gender lost. Loss of Grammatical Gender
Word Order Freer word order due to rich inflections. Fixed S-V-O word order became essential for meaning. Shift to Fixed S-V-O Order

II. Vocabulary (Lexicon)

The influx of French words following the Norman Conquest (1066).

Source Old English (c. 450–1150) Middle English (c. 1150–1500) Impact Summary
French (Anglo-Norman) Vocabulary almost entirely Germanic. Massive intake of words (law, politics, fine arts, religion). Lexical Expansion
Old Norse Already had significant influence in Northern dialects. Continued absorption of common words (e.g., *sky, egg*). Integration of Common Terms
Native Words Core of the language. Many native words lost or replaced by French synonyms. Loss of Native Lexemes

III. Sound & Spelling (Phonology & Orthography)

Changes in pronunciation and the loss of unique Germanic letters.

Feature Old English (c. 450–1150) Middle English (c. 1150–1500) Change Summary
Special Letters Used unique Germanic letters: **Þ** (Thorn) and **Ð** (Eth). **Þ/Ð** were replaced by the digraph **th**. New letters (K, J, V) adopted. Orthographic Shift
Vowel Endings Unstressed final vowels were generally pronounced. Unstressed final vowels often reduced or lost (Loss of Final -e). Vowel Reduction
Pronunciation Vowels pronounced closer to continental European languages. The **Great Vowel Shift** began in Late ME, changing long vowel sounds. Major Vowel Changes Initiated

The transition was largely driven by the Norman Conquest (1066), leading to language contact and linguistic simplification.
